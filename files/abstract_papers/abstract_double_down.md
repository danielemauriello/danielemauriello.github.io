Should investors buy a stock when it falls in value? Normatively, the answer should not depend on whether they already own it. Yet we document a robust behavioral regularity suggesting otherwise: investors are substantially more likely to purchase additional shares of a poorly performing stock when they already hold it. We establish this pattern using trading records from nearly three million transactions. Specifically, monthly returns in the six months preceding repurchases of already-owned stocks are 4 to 7.3 percentage points lower than those for newly acquired stocks. Additionally, while investors tend to be momentum seekers for non-owned stocks, a recent negative return raises the baseline probability of repurchasing an already-owned stock by approximately 24 percent. We propose motivated reasoning as a contributing mechanism: to avoid the psychological cost of realizing a loss and to preserve a self-image as a competent investor, individuals form optimistic beliefs that justify doubling down on underperforming assets they have actively chosen. To test this, we run a two-session experiment in which 700 participants invest in a risky asset, experience a negative return, and are then randomly assigned either to retain ownership or have the asset automatically liquidated. This design allows us to test whether ownership fosters optimistic recovery beliefs and doubling-down behavior. We further randomize the order in which beliefs and investment decisions are elicited, allowing us to test whether prompting individuals to think about the asset's return before acting can serve as a debiasing nudge, attenuating the influence of ownership-driven motivated reasoning on subsequent investment choices.
