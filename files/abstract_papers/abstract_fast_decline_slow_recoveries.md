Time series graphs are ubiquitous in modern life, making their accurate interpretation essential for informed decision-making. While a large literature documents negativity bias in judgments of levels, probabilities, and outcomes, little is known about whether people perceive the pace of positive and negative change differently. We investigate whether individuals systematically perceive negative economic changes as unfolding faster than positive ones. We run two online experiments with a total of 2,000 participants who judge the steepness of increases and decreases in randomly generated time series. To isolate perceptual bias from graphical properties, each graph is shown both in its original form and as a mirror image, holding all visual features constant while reversing the direction of change. We further examine individual correlates of the bias and experimentally manipulate whether participants attend to the economic meaning or the geometric properties of the graphs. We find robust evidence that participants perceive negative changes as faster than positive ones. This asymmetry is largely unrelated to observable individual characteristics, suggesting it reflects a broadly shared perceptual tendency. Directing attention toward geometric features attenuates the bias, consistent with the interpretation that contextual readings of economic dynamics drive asymmetric perception. These findings suggest that simple design interventions could serve as low-cost tools for reducing distorted public perceptions of economic change.
