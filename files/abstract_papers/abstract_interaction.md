In everyday life, we are subject to several well-documented biases stemming from bounded rationality and motivated reasoning. While these biases have been extensively studied in isolation, much less is known about whether—and how—they interact. This paper examines the relationship between motivated reasoning and bounded rationality, focusing on motivated errors and correlation neglect. We study whether, in settings where correlation neglect could arise, reliance on this heuristic provides individuals with convenient justifications for reaching desired conclusions—and conversely, whether motivational forces help debias individuals when the heuristic does not deliver the desired outcome. We investigate this using an experiment with a 2x2 factorial design in which 1,000 subjects complete a simple estimation task. Both the presence of a directional motive and the opportunity for correlation neglect to occur are exogenously manipulated. Preliminary results reveal no evidence of a compounding between the two biases. Instead, motivated reasoning and correlation neglect appear to crowd each other out: when exposed to both forces simultaneously, individuals tend to abandon one bias in favor of the other.
